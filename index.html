Crystal Hudson faces accusations of lenience in re-election campaign

By Reagin von Lehe

District 35 Democratic Councilmember Crystal Hudson is running for re-election this year with affordability at the forefront of her campaign in Crown Heights, Prospect Heights, Fort Greene and Clinton Hill residents.

But, her challengers are campaigning with promises of more aggressive affordable housing production, tougher policing and stronger community oversight.

Affordability and anti-displacement

The escalating pressures of gentrification has motivated Hudson to continue prioritizing affordability of housing, utilities, childcare and taxes in her re-election campaign.

Over two decades, the white population in District 35 tripled, while the Black population decreased by 40%, according to Hudson’s office.

Households in the area are experiencing a significant rent burden, spending over 30% of their income on rent. Evictions have been notably high in parts of Crown Heights and Fort Greene since 2019.

“We are not talking about gentrification now—gentrification has already come and maybe already gone,” Hudson said. “Everybody is struggling—nothing is affordable.”

Hudson’s has prioritized preserving tenant protections, resisting piecemeal rezonings without community input, and promoting a coordinated, large-scale affordable housing plan.

She opposed a proposal for a 150-unit, mixed-use development at 962 Pacific Street, arguing that such projects should wait until they align with a broader neighborhood rezoning plan.

She introduced legislation like the senior citizen and disability rent increase exemptions.

Climate and sustainability policies

Hudson doesn’t emphasize climate change as her signature issue. But, she has a history of supporting the integration of green building standards and investing in heat mitigation and public transit and bike infrastructure to reduce emissions in her district.

Reproductive health and social services

Hudson has progressive stances on reproductive health, supporting accessible contraception, protection of legal abortion and expanding maternal health services in underserved neighborhoods.

She has also championed social investments for Black communities through her Black Agenda, which includes health equity, mental health access, and community-based support.

Background and challenges ahead

As a third-generation Brooklynite and the daughter of Jamaican and Honduran immigrants, Hudson became the first openly gay Black woman elected to the New York City Council in January 2022. Caring for her mother with Alzheimer’s motivated her political career, pushing her to navigate and challenge public systems.

But, some believe Hudson has too moderate of an approach to rezonings, which has potentially slowed development, as she faces Dion Michael Ashman, Hector Robertson, and Kenny Lever in this year’s election.

Voters in Crown Heights should be on lookout for:

  • Hudson pushing a multi-neighborhood rezoning plan that ensures affordable housing rather than approving isolated projects.
  • Stronger tenant protections and exemptions.
  • Establishing better climate resilience and green infrastructure in historically underinvested blocks.
  • Stronger oversight of city agencies and development resistance that may displace long-term residents.

Nonetheless, Hudson is still viewed as the most politically experienced in the field, with more potential influence in Council leadership if re-elected.
